# 10 Study Techniques That Actually Work for College Students

Are you tired of cramming the night before exams and forgetting everything the next week? You're not alone. In this comprehensive guide, we'll explore 10 scientifically-proven study techniques that actually help you learn and retain information.

## 1. Spaced Repetition: The Science of Memory

Spaced repetition is one of the most powerful learning techniques backed by cognitive science. Instead of cramming, you review material at increasing intervals.

**How it works:**
- Review new information after 1 day
- Review again after 3 days
- Then after 7 days
- Finally after 14 days

## 2. The Feynman Technique

Named after Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, this technique forces you to truly understand what you're learning.

**The 4 steps:**
1. Choose a concept you want to learn
2. Explain it in simple terms (as if teaching a child)
3. Identify gaps in your understanding
4. Review and simplify further

## 3. Active Recall

Stop re-reading your notes passively. Active recall means testing yourself on the material without looking at your notes.

**Why it works:** Retrieval practice strengthens memory pathways in your brain.

## 4. Pomodoro Technique

Work in focused 25-minute intervals with 5-minute breaks.

**Benefits:**
- Maintains focus
- Prevents burnout
- Creates urgency

## 5. Interleaving

Mix up different subjects or topics in a single study session instead of blocking by subject.

**Example:** Study math for 30 mins, then biology for 30 mins, then history—rather than 2 hours of just math.

## 6. Create Mind Maps

Visual learners rejoice! Mind mapping helps you see connections between concepts.

## 7. Teach Someone Else

The best way to learn is to teach. Explain concepts to classmates or even your pet!

## 8. Use the SQ3R Method

- **Survey:** Skim the material
- **Question:** Turn headings into questions
- **Read:** Read actively to answer questions
- **Recite:** Summarize in your own words
- **Review:** Review your notes regularly

## 9. Cornell Note-Taking System

Divide your page into three sections:
- Notes column (right)
- Cue column (left)
- Summary (bottom)

## 10. Practice Testing

Take practice tests under exam conditions. This reduces test anxiety and improves recall.
